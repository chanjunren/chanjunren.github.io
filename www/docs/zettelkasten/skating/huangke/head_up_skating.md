# head_up_skating

> 抬头远望 — look far ahead, not at the cones. Improves balance, builds cone sense, looks better, and is safer.

## Why we look down

- **Unfamiliarity** with cone spacing → need visual confirmation of where each cone is
- This is a 桩感 (cone sense) problem, not a technique problem
- As cone sense improves, the need to look decreases

## Why looking down hurts

### Posture
- Head down → spine curves → increases spinal pressure
- Prolonged: headaches, dizziness, poor blood circulation

### Movement quality
- Attention locks onto feet → **upper body neglected**
- Creates 高低肩 (uneven shoulders) → imbalance → loss of control
- Moves look ugly because the body contorts to compensate

### Safety
- Can't see pedestrians, obstacles, or other skaters ahead
- Collision risk increases significantly

## Why looking up helps

### Stability
- **Locked head position** → trunk rotation stays small
- Less trunk rotation during fast skating = less risk of losing control
- Body becomes more controllable and predictable

### Cone sense development
- Force yourself to rely on **peripheral vision and body feel** instead of direct sight
- Single-cone moves: 45-degree downward glance is enough
- Moving through cones: look in your **direction of travel** (even if skating backwards, look where you're going)
- Over time, you internalize cone spacing — this is 桩感

### Looks better
- Audience and camera see a confident, composed skater
- vs. hunched-over fiddling that screams "struggling"

## How to practice

- Start with familiar moves you can already do eyes-closed
- Gradually extend to newer moves
- Target: peripheral vision catches the cone, direct gaze stays forward
- The farther ahead you can look while still weaving cleanly, the better your cone sense
