# double_fish

> 双鱼 — the most fundamental parallel-foot S-curve slalom move. Both feet stay together, weaving through cones in an S-line. Looks simple; hides deep mechanics.

## Three core requirements

1. **Feet parallel** — always side by side
2. **Body faces forward** — upper body stays front-facing and still
3. **S-curve path** through cones

## The turning secret

- Common mistake: pushing with feet (蹬) → looks like a frog flailing
- Actual mechanism: **waist/hip turning** (腰胯转向)
- Imagine being a puppet controlled only at the waist — legs follow
- If your waist is sore after a set, you're probably doing it right

## Force mechanics

- **Outer foot** provides the primary lateral push — nearly perpendicular to the cone line
- Waist/hip contributes supplementary force
- At zero speed: combined force vector points diagonally upward-forward
- At skating speed: force is almost purely **lateral** (横向)
- Knees slightly bent → lower center of gravity → more stability

## Why it matters

- Foundation of **all S-curve movements** in slalom
- Progression: 双鱼 → single-foot S-curve → single-wheel forward weaving
- Trains **core strength** and weight transfer control
- The "invisible base" that makes advanced moves possible

## Extensions via [[movement_extension_method]]

- **Element addition**: add forward/backward turning → continuous direction-change fish
- **Element substitution**: body forward → body sideways → "摆摆" (side-fish)
- **Deconstruction**: all-directional body rotation while maintaining parallel feet → "鱼转" (fish spin)
