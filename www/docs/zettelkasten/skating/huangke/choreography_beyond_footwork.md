# choreography_beyond_footwork

> Editing combos? Stop focusing only on feet. Body orientation, rise/fall, and cone-line position are more powerful musical tools.

## The footwork trap

Most skaters choreograph by arranging **foot tricks** in sequence, then try to "hit" musical beats with specific moves. Problem:
- Beat-matching with foot tricks **breaks sliding continuity**
- Quality and flow cliff-dive just to land one hit
- The combo becomes a list of moves, not a musical expression

## Musical tools that don't interrupt footwork

### Body orientation (身体朝向)
- Face the cone line, face away, face sideways — each reads differently
- Changing body direction **on a musical change** creates a visual accent without disrupting feet

### Rise and fall (身形起伏)
- Standing tall vs. crouching low
- Rising on a musical build, dropping on a beat — audience reads this as musical response
- Doesn't require any change in foot pattern

### Position relative to cone line (位置关系)
- Inside the cones, outside, crossing through
- Spatial shifts on musical phrases feel intentional
- Especially powerful at phrase boundaries

### Sliding speed, arc, and force (速度/弧度/力度)
- Speed up during a chorus, slow down during a verse
- Wider arcs for dramatic moments, tight arcs for precision
- These affect **律动** (groove) more than any foot trick

## The framework approach

Build a **container** using the tools above, then fill it with whatever footwork fits:
- The container handles musical connection
- Footwork handles visual complexity
- Result: more combos work, because the music-matching isn't load-bearing on the feet

## For improvisation too

These tools work as **助攻条件** (support conditions) for improv:
- When you don't know what to do with your feet — change body direction
- When the music shifts — drop your center of gravity
- See [[improvisation_flow_state]] for how this fits the flow template
