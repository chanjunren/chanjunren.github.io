# improvisation_subtraction

> 做减法 — reduce visible effort, hide force points, sustain flow with fewer pushes. The art of looking effortless.

## The problem with "addition"

Most skaters approach improv by **stuffing moves into the music** — constant weaving, lots of cone tricks, filling every beat. Result:
- Looks **chaotic** (瞎滑) from an audience perspective
- Can't truly enter an open, free-flowing state
- Disconnected from the music — moves fill space but don't respond to the song

## What subtraction means

### Hide force points (隐藏发力点)
- Reduce the number of times you visibly push, brake, or reverse direction
- One push → sustain flow through multiple cones
- **Unidirectional force** (顺向发力): movements that keep going in one direction naturally — snakes are the archetype

### Reduce reversals (减少逆向折返)
- Every reversal requires a visible push that breaks flow
- Prefer movements that chain **in the same direction**
- Smooth unidirectional → seamless transitions → "丝滑" (silky)

### Fewer, bigger moves
- Instead of many small cone tricks, use **整体动作** (whole-body movements)
- A few high-impact moves > many fragmented ones
- Let the music breathe — not every beat needs a trick

## Equipment hack for learning subtraction

- Switch to a **heavier frame** (e.g., from 魅影 to V-frame)
- Heavier = clearer force feedback per movement
- Forces you to be deliberate — can't spam small moves
- The weight makes every action's feedback more tangible

## Relationship to music

- With fewer moves, each move gets more musical weight
- Prioritize the **overall arc** (大盘) — your interpretation of the song's mood
- Rock → aggressive, rebellious movements
- Jazz → wide, swinging feel
- Pop → rhythmic, syncopated hits
- See [[choreo]] and [[rhythm]] for music fundamentals
