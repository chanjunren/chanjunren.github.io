# relative_force

> 相对发力 — both legs move in opposite directions (front/back) simultaneously, creating smooth, flowing slalom transitions.

## Why it matters

- Creates **visual tension and speed** through contrast — one leg forward while the other pulls back
- **Hides effort**: the opposing motion looks effortless because forces cancel out visible jolts
- Strengthens **hip flexibility** and **thigh explosiveness**
- Enhances **桩感** (cone sense) — the more relative force you use, the more fluid your weaving becomes

## How it works

- Keep **waist and hips stable** — they're the anchor
- **Thighs are the force points**, not feet or ankles
- Legs slide front/back relative to each other, like scissors
- Upper body stays relaxed and still

## Practice moves (best to worst for learning)

### 前后舌 (forward/backward tongue)
- Forward tongue: outer-eight and inner-eight variants
- Backward tongue: same variants
- Do these in 速桩 (speed cone) format to amplify the feeling

### 插花 (cross-step)
- **Best single move** for thigh explosiveness and relative force perception
- Almost entirely driven by opposing leg motion

### Brush
- Nearly 100% dual-leg force — minimizes reliance on momentum/inertia
- See [[brush]] for the full move breakdown

## Where to apply

Any move where both legs have **relative displacement** during sliding:
- Crazy, scissors, cross-steps, Nelson transitions
- Especially powerful in combo transitions — makes the "行云流水" (flowing water) visual effect
