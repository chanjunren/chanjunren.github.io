# compression

**Compression** is the foundation of balance in slalom skating: putting all weight fully and steadily onto the foot in contact with the ground. Balance in slalom isn't a vague feeling — it's specifically the act of compressing weight through the foot to the ground and maintaining a stable posture.

Without it, skating feels like standing on a rocking boat — dynamic movement becomes impossible to control.

## Direction Alignment

The direction of compression must align with the direction of the skate frame. Misalignment is what turns a T-brake into an uncontrolled C-turn: weight gets applied incorrectly across both feet, causing the legs to rotate along the frame direction unintentionally.

**Exception: Slide** — the only technique where the compressed foot moves *perpendicular* to the frame direction. The foot still bears full weight, which is what distinguishes a slide from dragging (where the braking foot bears no weight).

## Signs of Poor Compression

- One foot lifts during movement when it shouldn't
- Loss of stability mid-trick
- Skating feels effortful and reactive rather than fluid

Developing compression sense early is essential — it underlies both beginner control and advanced fluidity. See [[pressure]] for the push force that builds on top of this.
